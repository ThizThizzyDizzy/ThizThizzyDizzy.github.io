Overhaul Solid-Fuel Fission Reactors

External Structure

Overhaul SFRs are built with a cuboidal casing. The edges must be Reactor Casings, but the faces can also be made with Reactor Glass.
Fuel can be piped in/out via Fission Fuel Cell Ports placed in the casing.
Coolant can be piped in/out via Fission Reactor Vents.
Right click any port or vent to switch in between Input and Output mode. In output mode, they will push their contents to adjacent pipes or inventories. (Do not use servos or similar, as they will limit the output rate)
Items from irradiator recipes can be piped in/out via a Neutron Irradiator Port.
Neutron sources are placed in the casing. (seen behind the levers above)
To turn on a reactor, provide it with fuel, coolant, and a redstone signal to all neutron sources.
To turn off a reactor, the design must include Neutron Shields, which can be toggled with a Fission Neutron Shield Manager. (see Neutron Shields below for more info) Alternatively you can let the fuel cells run out of fuel. This might lead to meltdowns if not all of them run out at the same time though.

Fuels

Fuel Pellets are inserted via a Fission Fuel Cell Port.
Each fuel has 4 stats:
Efficiency: The base efficiency of the fuel
Base Heat: The base heat generation of the fuel
Criticality: The amount of neutron flux required to activate a fuel cell
Base Time: The time, in ticks, that one fuel pellet will last in one fuel cell
Some fuels are also self-priming, so they do not require neutron sources.

Internal Structure

Every Fission reactor contains Fuel Cell, Moderators, and Heat sinks. They may also contain Reflectors, Irradiators, Neutron Shields, and Conductors.

Fuel Cells

Each fuel cell can have a different fuel. To filter a fuel cell or port, right click with a fuel pellet (filtered cells are accessed through filtered ports)
Fuel cells must be activated for the reactor to start. They are activated, when they receive enough neutron flux. (when flux >= fuel criticality)
Neutron flux spreads through the reactor's core through moderator lines. Neutron flux originates from cells that are primed with a neutron source. To prime a cell, place a neutron source on the casing with line-of-sight to the cell you want to prime. (Line-of-sight is obstructed by cells, reflectors, and irradiators, but not by heat sinks, moderators, or conductors)
You can also prime a cell in the planner by shift-clicking it.

Once a cell is activated, it will transmit neutron flux through all its moderator lines. Fuel cells will not stay active unless they have enough sustained neutron flux from other active cells.
Cells gain a heat multiplier and efficiency multiplier from all connected moderator lines. The heat multiplier is equal to the number of adjacent moderator lines, while the positional efficiency is the sum of each line's average efficiency.
The total efficiency multiplier is the positional efficiency multiplied by fuel efficiency.

Moderators

Moderators transmit neutron flux between Fuel Cells, Reflectors, and Irradiators. Each moderator has a Flux factor and an Efficiency factor. Moderator lines can be a maximum of 4 blocks long. Here is a list of all moderators' stats:
Moderator Flux Factor Efficiency Factor
Graphite Moderator 10 1.1
Beryllium Moderator 22 1.05
Heavy Water Moderator 36 1
Actual values may vary based on mod configuration.

The total flux of a moderator line is the sum of each moderator's flux factor.
The total efficiency of a moderator line is the average of each moderator's efficiency factor.

Reflectors

Reflectors will reflect neutron flux back to cells through moderator lines.
Each reflector has a Reflectivity and Efficiency factor.
Moderator lines to reflectors can only be 2 blocks long, as the neutron flux is traveling to the reflector, then back. (a total of 4 blocks)
The Neutron flux of these moderators is also doubled for the same reason.
The moderator line's neutron flux is then multiplied by the reflector's reflectivity.
The moderator line's efficiency is also multiplied by the reflector's efficiency.
Here is a list of all reflectors' stats:
Reflector Reflectivity Efficiency Factor
Lead-Steel 0.5 0.25
Beryllium-Carbon 1 0.5
Actual values may vary based on mod configuration.

Irradiators

Irradiators are placed in a reactor to perform recipes using neutron flux. Irradiators are placed at the end of a moderator line. Each irradiator recipe requires a specific amount of neutrons, so more neutron flux will speed up the recipe. (although one moderator is enough for a very slow irradiator)
For example:

Each Irradiator recipe has an efficiency multiplier. The moderator line's efficiency is multiplied by the irradiator recipe's efficiency multiplier.
Irradiator recipes can also produce heat. If so, the irradiator will generate heat for each point of neutron flux provided to the irradiator.

Neutron Shields

Neutron shields can be used to toggle moderator lines and shut down reactors. Neutron shields can be placed in place of any moderator, though they have a neutron flux of 0. The shield efficiency is also used as moderator efficiency.
Here is a list of all shields' stats:
Shield Efficiency Heat Per Flux
Boron-Silver 0.5 5
Shields generate heat for each point of neutron flux passing through them. Only some of this heat will count towards heating coolant. (Heat*shield efficiency) Shields can be closed with a Shield Controller, which will shut downs its moderator line, blocking neutron flux from neighboring cells. If a cell's flux falls below its criticality, it will shut down as well.
For example:



The left shield will generate 100 heat, from 20 flux passing through it. (10 on the way to the reflector, 10 on the way back)
The middle shield will generate 150 heat, from the 30 flux passing through it. (20 from the left, 10 from the right)
The right shield will generate 180 heat, from the 36 flux passing through it. (the moderators to its right do not provide it flux, as the irradiator absorbs it)

There are a few types of placement rules:
'2 Fuel Cells' requires 2 or more fuel cells directly adjacent to this heat sink.
'Exactly 2 Redstone Heat Sinks' requires exactly 2 adjacent Redstone Heat Sink.
'Axial Lapis Heat Sinks' requires 2 lapis heat sinks on opposite sides. (There can be more than 2 lapis heat sinks, but at least 2 of them must be on opposite sides)
'Exactly-Axial Lead Heat Sinks' requires exactly 2 lead heat sinks on opposite sides. (There can not be more than 2 adjacent lead heat sinks)
Note that you cannot have casing blocks inside the reactor; Heat Sinks that require casings must be on the outer edges of the reactor.

Clusters

Clusters are groups of cells, irradiators, shields, heat sinks that are connected together. Every cluster must be connected to the casing. Clusters can be connected to each other and to the casing with Conductors.
If a cluster has a net of >10, all its fuel cells will run through fuel faster than normal. (The cluster will also melt down once its heat buffer fills up)
If a cluster has a net heat of <-10, it will get an extra penalty to efficiency.
Actual values may vary based on mod configuration.

Sparsity Penalty

If the reactor is less than 75% full of functional blocks, it will get an extra penalty to efficiency. Functional blocks include all valid cells, moderators, reflectors, irradiators, shields, and heat sinks, but not conductors.
Actual values may vary based on mod configuration.
